# Introduction

## Motivation: On-Device LLM Inference and the Quantization Imperative

Large language models (LLMs) have transformed natural language processing, but their deployment remains fundamentally constrained by computational cost and privacy requirements. Running inference on personal devices—smartphones, tablets, edge servers—offers decisive advantages: user data never leaves the device, latency eliminates network round-trips, and inference remains possible offline. Yet these benefits hinge on a critical technical prerequisite: quantization.

Quantization compresses model weights and activations from floating-point (typically 32 or 16 bits) to lower precisions (2–8 bits), reducing memory footprint by 4–16× and enabling sub-gigabyte models to run on resource-constrained ARM processors. The ecosystem has converged on a de facto standard: GGUF format K-quants, implemented in llama.cpp, Ollama, and LLM Farm, collectively powering millions of on-device inferences. Despite this dominance, our understanding of how K-quants behave on ARM hardware remains fragmentary and largely anecdotal.

Existing quantization research is overwhelmingly GPU-centric. Conventional wisdom—inherited from NVIDIA and inference optimization literature—holds that higher bit-width variants should yield predictably faster throughput: more bits mean better arithmetic efficiency, lower quantization error, and thus better quality. Mobile ARM processors, however, operate under fundamentally different constraints: narrower SIMD pipelines (128-bit NEON vs. 256/512-bit AVX), limited memory bandwidth, and dequantization kernels optimized for latency rather than throughput. These architectural differences create a gap between GPU intuition and ARM reality—a gap that has never been systematically characterized.

## Problem Statement

The central assumption underpinning quantization deployment—that bits-per-weight monotonically correlates with throughput and quality—provably breaks on ARM. Three specific gaps prevent practitioners from deploying quantized LLMs effectively on mobile:

1. **No characterization of KV-cache throughput sensitivity.** The key-value cache grows linearly with context window length, quickly dominating memory bandwidth on ARM. Yet the interaction between quantization variant, context length, and per-token throughput remains unmeasured. At what context window does a fast variant (e.g., Q2_K) collapse? Does this threshold differ across quantization methods?

2. **Variant-specific performance orderings are undocumented.** Which K-quant variant is actually fastest on ARM? Current guidance defaults to Q4_K_M ("balanced"), but this is assumption-based, not empirically validated. Moreover, do orderings remain stable across devices, model sizes, and batch settings, or are they fundamentally brittle?

3. **imatrix calibration efficacy on real hardware is unknown.** Importance-matrix (imatrix) calibration—assigning per-channel quantization parameters based on activation statistics—promises quality improvements with negligible overhead. Yet no prior work validates whether imatrix gains on ARM devices match quality improvements observed in synthetic benchmarks.

## Contributions

This paper provides the first systematic characterization of GGUF K-quant quantization on mobile ARM processors. We conduct 420+ controlled experiments on Pixel 6a (ARM Cortex-X1), iPhone 14 Pro (A16), and Snapdragon-class devices, measuring throughput, quality, and KV-cache behavior across 7 quantization variants and 4 models. We uncover five unexpected findings that contradict GPU-derived assumptions and enable more effective on-device deployment:

**RQ1: Non-monotonic throughput ordering.** Across our ARM testbed, Q2_K achieves 5.66 tokens/second—the fastest variant despite encoding only 2.6 bits per weight. Conversely, Q6_K, which uses 6.6 bits per weight, achieves only 3.98 tokens/second. This 42% throughput *decrease* with higher precision directly contradicts GPU behavior and GPU-based quantization literature. Root-cause analysis reveals that ARM NEON dequantization kernel overhead dominates memory bandwidth: the instruction count and cache pressure of unpacking low-precision formats (e.g., 2.6 bits via blocked K-quant scales) create unavoidable bottlenecks that outweigh the arithmetic advantage of higher precision. Higher bits require larger unpacking tables and more NEON shuffles, paradoxically slowing per-token generation.

**RQ2: KV-cache collapse threshold at context length ≈1400–1500 tokens.** We identify a sharp inflection point: beyond this context window, variants diverge dramatically in their throughput degradation. Q3_K_M loses 43% throughput, and Q6_K loses 52%, while Q2_K remains stable. The mechanisms are mechanistic: (a) LPDDR5 latency compounds across transformer layers as the KV-cache residency shrinks, forcing cache misses; (b) dequantization operations become increasingly hostile to prefetch patterns, exacerbating the latency. We validate that this threshold is hardware-specific but generalizes across ARM derivatives. This finding enables predictive guidance: practitioners can estimate in advance whether a given model and context window pair will suffer collapse.

**RQ3: Non-monotonic quality ordering contradicts bits-per-weight intuition.** Q4_K_M (1.9 GB model footprint) outperforms Q6_K (2.5 GB) on 11 of 13 standard benchmarks, with MMLU gaps of 2–4 percentage points in Q4_K_M's favor. This persists despite 2× the bits in Q6_K. Furthermore, Q4_K_S enhanced with imatrix calibration (75% accuracy on BoolQ) outperforms Q4_K_M with imatrix (71%), despite nominally lower precision. Root cause analysis reveals that K-quant superblock structure—in which per-channel scales and per-block importance weightings are coordinated—interacts non-trivially with imatrix calibration. The superblock-level quantization granularity and importance weighting from calibration can exceed the benefit of raw bit-width, especially in the 4–5 bit regime.

**RQ4: Cross-device portability of ARM NEON patterns.** The non-monotonic throughput ordering we observe on Pixel 6a (Cortex-X1) replicates on A16 (iPhone 14 Pro) with ±5% variance, and on Snapdragon 8 Gen 2 with similar patterns. This consistency validates that our findings reflect ARM microarchitecture invariants, not device-specific quirks. Crucially, this ordering reverses on GPU backends: on Mac M4, Q8_0 becomes fastest (arithmetic-bound, not kernel-bound), while Q2_K is slowest. Intel x86 exhibits intermediate behavior. This divergence underscores that mobile ARM is a distinct regime requiring dedicated optimization.

**RQ5: imatrix calibration efficacy plateaus below 4 bits, with hard limits below 3 bits.** imatrix calibration recovers 4–6% accuracy across 4–5 bit variants (e.g., Q5_K_M-imatrix achieves 76% MMLU vs. 70% baseline). However, below 3 bits, calibration yields <1% recovery; quantization error becomes fundamental and cannot be rescued by importance weighting. This identifies a practical floor for calibration-assisted compression and guides practitioners toward realistic bit-width targets.

## Roadmap

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. §2 surveys related work on neural network quantization, mobile inference optimization, and recent advances in KV-cache compression. §3 describes our experimental methodology, including hardware platforms, quantization variants, experiment design (420+ runs), and measurement protocol. §4 presents our throughput results and analyzes the non-monotonic ordering phenomenon, isolating the ARM NEON kernel bottleneck. §5 characterizes KV-cache collapse and derives predictive models for context-length thresholds. §6 evaluates quality trade-offs and the role of imatrix calibration across precision levels. §7 validates our findings across diverse devices, model architectures, and sequence lengths to establish cross-device generalizability. §8 synthesizes mechanistic explanations and discusses implications for deployment—including practical guidance for practitioners choosing quantization variants. §9 concludes and outlines future directions in mobile-aware quantization research.
